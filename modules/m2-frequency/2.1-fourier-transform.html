2.1 — Signal Decomposition & the Fourier Transform

Module 2 · Frequency Domain · ~25 min

Introduction

Imagine looking at ocean waves from above. You see a complex, chaotic surface. Now imagine a magical prism that separates the ocean into all its constituent wave components — long swells, medium chop, tiny ripples — each at its own frequency. You could then see exactly how much energy is at each scale.

The Fourier Transform is that prism for signals. It decomposes any signal into its constituent sinusoidal components, revealing the frequency spectrum — what frequencies are present and how strongly.

💡 The Big Idea Any signal can be expressed as a sum (possibly infinite) of sine and cosine waves at different frequencies. The Fourier Transform computes the amplitude and phase of each frequency component.

Core Concept

From Time Domain to Frequency Domain

The time domain shows how a signal changes over time. The frequency domain shows which sinusoidal frequencies are present and at what strength. These are two complementary views of the same signal.

Spectrum: Magnitude and Phase

The Fourier Transform of a real signal produces complex numbers at each frequency. We typically show:

  • Magnitude spectrum $|X(f)|$ — how much of each frequency is present
  • Phase spectrum $\angle X(f)$ — the phase shift of each frequency component

Real vs. Complex Signals

For real signals (which most physical signals are), the magnitude spectrum is symmetric (even) and the phase spectrum is antisymmetric (odd) about $f = 0$. This means you only need to look at positive frequencies — the negative side carries no new information.

Mathematical Foundation

Continuous Fourier Transform (CFT)

The Fourier Transform of a continuous signal $x(t)$:

$$X(f) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t) \, e^{-j2\pi ft} \, dt$$

And the inverse (reconstruction from spectrum):

$$x(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} X(f) \, e^{j2\pi ft} \, df$$

where $j = \sqrt{-1}$ and $e^{j\theta} = \cos\theta + j\sin\theta$ (Euler's formula).

Intuition: $e^{-j2\pi ft}$ is a complex sinusoid spinning at frequency $f$. The integral measures how much of that spinning pattern is present in $x(t)$. If $x(t)$ contains a lot of frequency $f$, the integral is large; if not, it's near zero.

Discrete-Time Fourier Transform (DTFT)

For discrete signals $x[n]$:

$$X(e^{j\hat\omega}) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x[n] \, e^{-j\hat\omega n}$$

where $\hat\omega \in [-\pi, \pi]$ is the digital frequency (radians/sample). This produces a continuous, periodic spectrum.

Parseval's Theorem (Energy Conservation)

The total energy is the same in both domains:

$$\sum_{n} |x[n]|^2 = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} |X(e^{j\hat\omega})|^2 \, d\hat\omega$$

Conceptual Questions

Q1. What does the Fourier Transform fundamentally tell you about a signal?

  • How the signal changes from one sample to the next
  • Which frequencies are present and at what amplitude/phase
  • The sampling rate needed to record the signal
  • The total energy in decibels

Q2. For a real signal, the magnitude spectrum is:

  • Random and asymmetric
  • Symmetric (even) about f = 0
  • All zeros for negative frequencies
  • Only defined for positive frequencies

Q3. What does the magnitude spectrum of a pure 440 Hz sine wave look like?

  • A smooth hill centered at 440 Hz
  • A single spike (delta function) at 440 Hz (and -440 Hz)
  • A flat line across all frequencies
  • Many spikes at 440, 880, 1320, ... Hz

MATLAB Exercise

Compute and plot the magnitude spectrum of signals using MATLAB's fft().

%% 2.1 Fourier Transform with MATLAB

fs = 1000;          % Sampling frequency (Hz)
N  = 1024;          % Number of samples
t  = (0:N-1)/fs;    % Time vector
f  = (0:N-1)*fs/N;  % Frequency vector (one-sided: use 1:N/2)

%% Part A — Single sine wave
f1 = 100;           % Signal frequency
x1 = sin(2*pi*f1*t);
X1 = fft(x1);       % Complex spectrum

figure('Name','FFT of Sine Wave');
subplot(2,1,1); plot(t, x1); xlabel('Time (s)'); ylabel('x[n]'); title('440 Hz sine — time domain'); xlim([0 0.05]);
subplot(2,1,2);
plot(f(1:N/2), abs(X1(1:N/2)) * 2/N);  % One-sided, normalized
xlabel('Frequency (Hz)'); ylabel('|X(f)|'); title('Magnitude spectrum'); xlim([0 fs/2]);

%% Part B — Sum of three sinusoids
f1 = 50; f2 = 120; f3 = 300;
A1 = 1.0; A2 = 0.6; A3 = 0.4;
x2 = A1*sin(2*pi*f1*t) + A2*sin(2*pi*f2*t) + A3*sin(2*pi*f3*t);
X2 = fft(x2);

figure('Name','Three Sinusoids');
subplot(2,1,1); plot(t, x2); xlabel('Time (s)'); title('Sum of 50, 120, 300 Hz sine waves'); xlim([0 0.1]);
subplot(2,1,2);
stem(f(1:N/2), abs(X2(1:N/2))*2/N, 'filled', 'MarkerSize', 3);
xlabel('Frequency (Hz)'); ylabel('|X(f)|'); title('Magnitude spectrum — 3 peaks visible'); xlim([0 fs/2]);

%% Part C — View both magnitude and phase
figure('Name','Magnitude and Phase');
subplot(2,1,1); plot(f(1:N/2), abs(X1(1:N/2))*2/N); title('Magnitude'); xlabel('Hz'); xlim([0 200]);
subplot(2,1,2); plot(f(1:N/2), angle(X1(1:N/2))); title('Phase (radians)'); xlabel('Hz'); xlim([0 200]);
💡 Hint — Why multiply by 2/N?

MATLAB's fft() returns raw complex values. To get the correct amplitude (in same units as the input), you divide by $N$ (because FFT sums N terms). For a one-sided spectrum (positive frequencies only), you multiply by 2 to account for the energy at negative frequencies. So normalized amplitude = abs(fft(x))*2/N.

Key Takeaways

  • The Fourier Transform decomposes a signal into sinusoidal components, revealing the frequency spectrum.
  • The output is complex; we usually plot magnitude $|X(f)|$ and phase $\angle X(f)$.
  • For real signals, the spectrum is symmetric about $f = 0$ — only positive frequencies carry new information.
  • In MATLAB: X = fft(x), normalize amplitude with abs(X)*2/N for one-sided plot.
  • Energy is preserved: $\sum|x[n]|^2 = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int|X|^2 d\omega$ (Parseval's theorem).
