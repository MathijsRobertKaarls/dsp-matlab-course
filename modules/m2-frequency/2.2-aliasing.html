2.2 — Aliasing (Spectral Foldback)

Module 2 · Frequency Domain · ~20 min

Introduction

You've seen a movie where a spinning wheel appears to rotate backward, or even stand still. This is an optical aliasing effect — the camera samples the wheel too slowly to capture its true rotation speed. The same phenomenon occurs in digital signal processing: sample a signal too slowly, and high-frequency components appear to "fold back" into the lower frequency range — completely disguised as lower frequencies.

This is aliasing, and it's a critical failure mode in DSP systems. Once it happens, the aliased frequencies are indistinguishable from legitimate ones — you cannot undo aliasing after the fact.

⚠ Aliasing is irreversible. Once a signal has been aliased, you cannot recover the original high-frequency content. Prevention (sampling fast enough, or using an anti-aliasing filter) is the only solution.

Core Concept

When Aliasing Occurs

Aliasing occurs whenever a signal contains frequencies at or above half the sampling frequency:

$$f_{signal} \geq \frac{f_s}{2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{aliasing!}$$

The Nyquist frequency $f_N = f_s/2$ is the maximum representable frequency.

Spectral Foldback

When a frequency $f$ exceeds $f_s/2$, it "folds back" and appears at the alias frequency:

$$f_{alias} = |f - k \cdot f_s|, \quad \text{where } k \text{ is chosen so that } f_{alias} \in [0, f_s/2]$$

For the simplest case of one alias:

$$f_{alias} = f_s - f \quad \text{(when } f_s/2 < f < f_s \text{)}$$
Example: $f_s = 1000$ Hz, signal frequency $f = 750$ Hz. The alias appears at $f_{alias} = 1000 - 750 = 250$ Hz. In your recording, a 750 Hz tone sounds like a 250 Hz tone!

Anti-Aliasing Filter

The standard solution is to place a lowpass filter (the anti-aliasing filter) before the ADC. This filter removes all signal content above $f_s/2$ before sampling, preventing the fold-back.

$$x_{filtered}(t) = x(t) * h_{LP}(t) \quad \text{where } h_{LP} \text{ cuts off at } f_s/2$$

Mathematical Foundation

Spectrum of a sampled signal

Sampling multiplies the signal by an impulse train. In the frequency domain, this creates periodic copies of the spectrum spaced $f_s$ apart:

$$X_s(f) = f_s \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} X(f - k f_s)$$

If the original spectrum $X(f)$ is bandlimited to $[-f_s/2, f_s/2]$, the copies don't overlap and the original can be perfectly recovered. If not, the copies overlap — that overlap is aliasing.

MATLAB Exercise

Observe aliasing by deliberately undersampling a tone and examining the FFT.

%% 2.2 Aliasing Demonstration

fs_true = 10000;   % High-rate "true" continuous signal
fs_low  = 1000;    % Undersampled rate (Nyquist = 500 Hz)
T = 0.5;

t_true = 0:1/fs_true:T;
t_low  = 0:1/fs_low:T;

f_signal = 750;    % ABOVE Nyquist (500 Hz) — will alias to 250 Hz

x_true = sin(2*pi*f_signal*t_true);
x_low  = sin(2*pi*f_signal*t_low);   % Undersampled version

%% Compare FFTs
N_true = length(x_true);
N_low  = length(x_low);

f_ax_true = (0:N_true-1)*fs_true/N_true;
f_ax_low  = (0:N_low-1)*fs_low/N_low;

figure('Name','Aliasing Demonstration');
subplot(2,2,1);
plot(t_true(1:200), x_true(1:200)); title('True 750 Hz signal');
xlabel('Time (s)'); ylim([-1.5 1.5]);

subplot(2,2,2);
stem(t_low(1:50), x_low(1:50), 'filled'); title(sprintf('Sampled at %d Hz (sparse)', fs_low));
xlabel('Time (s)'); ylim([-1.5 1.5]);

subplot(2,2,3);
plot(f_ax_true(1:N_true/2), abs(fft(x_true))*2/N_true);
title('Spectrum (true)'); xlabel('Hz'); xlim([0 1000]);
xline(f_signal, 'r--', '750 Hz');

subplot(2,2,4);
stem(f_ax_low(1:N_low/2), abs(fft(x_low))*2/N_low, 'filled');
title('Spectrum (aliased)'); xlabel('Hz'); xlim([0 500]);
xline(fs_low - f_signal, 'r--', sprintf('%d Hz alias', fs_low - f_signal));

%% Try different signal frequencies
fprintf('\nAlias prediction table (fs = %d Hz):\n', fs_low);
for f = [100 250 400 500 600 750 900]
  if f < fs_low/2
    alias = f;
    note = '(no alias, f < Nyquist)';
  else
    alias = abs(f - fs_low);
    note = sprintf('ALIAS at %d Hz', alias);
  end
  fprintf('  f = %4d Hz  →  %s\n', f, note);
end
💡 Hint — What to look for

In subplot (2,2,3) you'll see the spectral spike at 750 Hz — that's the true signal. In subplot (2,2,4), the spike has moved to 250 Hz — that's the alias! The sampled version looks like a 250 Hz signal. The terminal output will also print a table showing which frequencies alias and to where.

Key Takeaways

  • Aliasing occurs when $f_{signal} \geq f_s/2$: high frequencies fold back into the baseband.
  • Alias frequency: $f_{alias} = |f - k \cdot f_s|$ for the appropriate integer $k$.
  • Aliasing is irreversible — prevent it before ADC with a lowpass anti-aliasing filter.
  • The Nyquist frequency $f_s/2$ is the highest frequency your digital system can represent.
  • Real ADCs include built-in anti-aliasing filters (oversampling + digital filter).
