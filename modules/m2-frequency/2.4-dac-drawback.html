2.4 — Drawback of a DAC: Staircase & Sinc Roll-Off

Module 2 · Frequency Domain · ~20 min

Introduction

When a DAC converts digital samples back to an analog signal, it doesn't magically produce a smooth curve. Instead, it holds each sample value constant until the next sample arrives — creating a staircase waveform. This is called Zero-Order Hold (ZOH).

This staircase introduces a predictable distortion in the frequency domain: the high-frequency components of the signal are attenuated, following a sinc function shape. Understanding this effect — and how to compensate for it — is essential for building accurate DAC-based systems.

Core Concept

Zero-Order Hold (ZOH)

A DAC receives a digital value every $T_s$ seconds and outputs a constant voltage for that entire period. This creates a stepped approximation of the original signal:

Frequency Domain Effect: Sinc Attenuation

The ZOH operation is equivalent to convolving the impulse train of samples with a rectangular pulse of width $T_s$. In the frequency domain, this multiplication by the sinc function:

$$H_{ZOH}(f) = T_s \cdot \text{sinc}(f T_s) \cdot e^{-j\pi f T_s}$$

where $\text{sinc}(x) = \sin(\pi x)/(\pi x)$.

This means:

  • DC (f = 0): no attenuation (sinc(0) = 1) ✓
  • Near Nyquist ($f \approx f_s/2$): about 3.9 dB of attenuation
  • At $f = f_s$: complete null (sinc goes to 0)

Compensation: Sinc Correction Filter

To compensate for the ZOH droop, a sinc correction filter is applied — either in the digital domain (before the DAC) or as an analog filter after the DAC. Its response is the inverse of the ZOH:

$$H_{comp}(f) = \frac{1}{H_{ZOH}(f)} = \frac{1}{\text{sinc}(f/f_s)}$$

Mathematical Foundation

ZOH as convolution

The ZOH output is:

$$y_{ZOH}(t) = \sum_{n} x[n] \cdot \text{rect}\!\left(\frac{t - nT_s - T_s/2}{T_s}\right)$$

where $\text{rect}(u) = 1$ for $|u| \leq 1/2$, zero otherwise.

Frequency response

Taking the Fourier Transform, the ZOH frequency response magnitude is:

$$|H_{ZOH}(f)| = T_s \left|\frac{\sin(\pi f T_s)}{\pi f T_s}\right| = T_s \cdot |\text{sinc}(f T_s)|$$

At $f = f_s/2 = 1/(2T_s)$: $|H_{ZOH}| = T_s \cdot |\text{sinc}(1/2)| = T_s \cdot \frac{2}{\pi} \approx 0.637\,T_s$

This corresponds to $20\log_{10}(2/\pi) \approx -3.92$ dB of droop.

Q3. How can ZOH sinc roll-off be compensated?

MATLAB Exercise

Simulate the ZOH effect and visualize the sinc roll-off in the frequency domain.

%% 2.4 DAC Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) Demonstration

fs  = 1000;     % Original sampling rate
fs2 = 100;      % "DAC output rate" (simulate ZOH)
T   = 0.5;

t_orig = 0:1/fs:T;
t_zoh  = 0:1/fs2:T;

% Generate test signal: sum of tones
x = 0.5*sin(2*pi*50*t_orig) + 0.5*sin(2*pi*400*t_orig);

% Downsample to fs2 (simulate what DAC receives)
x_s = x(1:fs/fs2:end);

% ZOH: repeat each sample fs/fs2 times
x_zoh = repelem(x_s, fs/fs2);
x_zoh = x_zoh(1:length(t_orig));

%% Plot time domain comparison
figure('Name','ZOH Effect');
subplot(2,1,1);
plot(t_orig, x);
hold on; stairs(t_orig, x_zoh, 'r');
title('Original (blue) vs ZOH reconstruction (red)');
xlabel('Time (s)'); legend('Original','ZOH output');

%% Frequency domain comparison
N = length(x);
f_ax = (0:N/2-1)*fs/N;

X_orig = abs(fft(x))*2/N;
X_zoh  = abs(fft(x_zoh))*2/N;

subplot(2,1,2);
plot(f_ax, X_orig(1:N/2), 'b', 'DisplayName','Original');
hold on;
plot(f_ax, X_zoh(1:N/2),  'r', 'DisplayName','ZOH');
xlabel('Frequency (Hz)'); ylabel('|X(f)|');
title('Spectrum: note high-frequency droop in ZOH'); legend; xlim([0 fs/2]);

%% Theoretical ZOH frequency response
f = linspace(0, fs/2, 500);
T_s = 1/fs2;
H_zoh = abs(sinc(f * T_s));  % MATLAB sinc is normalized: sinc(x) = sin(pi*x)/(pi*x)

figure('Name','ZOH Frequency Response');
plot(f, 20*log10(H_zoh));
xlabel('Frequency (Hz)'); ylabel('Attenuation (dB)');
title(sprintf('ZOH Sinc Roll-Off (DAC rate = %d Hz)', fs2));
yline(-3.92, '--r', '-3.92 dB at Nyquist'); grid on; xlim([0 fs/2]);
💡 Hint

The 50 Hz component should appear almost identical in both spectra (near DC, ZOH barely attenuates). The 400 Hz component (close to Nyquist of 500 Hz) will be visibly lower in the ZOH spectrum. The theoretical plot shows the smooth sinc roll-off — you'll see the ~3.9 dB droop right at 500 Hz.

Key Takeaways

  • A DAC outputs a staircase (ZOH): each sample held constant for one period $T_s$.
  • ZOH is equivalent to multiplying the spectrum by $\text{sinc}(f/f_s)$ — highest frequencies are most attenuated.
  • Attenuation at Nyquist ($f_s/2$) is about 3.92 dB.
  • An inverse-sinc correction filter compensates for the ZOH droop.
  • Modern DACs use oversampling + digital interpolation to push ZOH distortion far above the audio band.
