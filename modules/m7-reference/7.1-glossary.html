7.1 — Glossary of Terms & Concepts

Module 7 · Reference & Summary · ~20 min

Introduction

This is the master glossary for the whole course. Every key term, name, and concept from Modules 1–6 is collected here with a plain-language meaning and a pointer back to the topic where it lives. Use it as a quick lookup while you work, or read it top-to-bottom as a final review. Terms are grouped by theme; within each group they roughly follow the order you met them.

How to use this page: skim the bold term, read the one-line meaning, and follow the "see" link if you need the full story. The companion pages 7.2 (equations), 7.3 (MATLAB functions), and 7.4 (lifehacks) cover the math, code, and tricks.

1 · Sampling & Signals

Term Meaning See
DSPDigital Signal Processing — analysing and modifying signals after they have been sampled into numbers.1.1
ADCAnalog-to-Digital Converter — turns a continuous voltage into discrete samples (sample + quantize).1.2
DACDigital-to-Analog Converter — reconstructs a continuous signal from samples (typically a zero-order hold).1.2, 2.4
SamplingMeasuring a signal at regular time intervals $T_s = 1/f_s$.1.2
Sampling rate $f_s$Number of samples taken per second (Hz). $T_s = 1/f_s$ is the sample period.1.2
Nyquist frequencyHalf the sample rate, $f_s/2$. The highest frequency that can be represented without aliasing.1.2, 2.2
Nyquist–Shannon theoremTo capture a signal exactly, sample at more than twice its highest frequency ($f_s > 2 f_{max}$).1.2, 2.2
QuantizationRounding each sample to the nearest level the ADC can store; introduces quantization noise.1.2, 3.1
Stationary signalA signal whose statistics (mean, variance, spectral content) don't change over time.1.3
Periodic signalRepeats exactly every period $T$: $x(t) = x(t+T)$.1.3
Quasi-stationaryApproximately stationary over a short window — the assumption behind the STFT.1.3, 4.1

2 · Systems

TermMeaningSee
SystemAnything that maps an input signal to an output signal.1.4
LinearObeys superposition: scaling and adding inputs scales and adds outputs.1.4
Time-invariantBehaviour doesn't change over time — a delayed input gives an equally delayed output.1.4
LTI systemLinear & Time-Invariant — fully described by its impulse response; the foundation of filter theory.1.4
CausalOutput depends only on present and past inputs — required for real-time processing.1.4
Impulse response $h[n]$A system's output to a single unit impulse $\delta[n]$; it characterizes an LTI system completely.1.4, 5.1
ConvolutionThe operation that produces an LTI system's output: $y = x * h$.1.4
Frequency response $H(e^{j\omega})$How an LTI system scales/shifts each frequency; the Fourier transform of $h[n]$.5.1, 5.5

3 · Frequency Domain

TermMeaningSee
Fourier Transform (FT)Decomposes a signal into the sine/cosine (frequency) components that make it up.2.1
SpectrumThe amount of each frequency present in a signal (magnitude) and its phase.2.1
DFTDiscrete Fourier Transform — the FT for a finite list of samples; $O(N^2)$ if done directly.2.3
FFTFast Fourier Transform — an $O(N\log N)$ algorithm that computes the exact same DFT.2.3
BinOne output point of the DFT; bin $k$ corresponds to frequency $k f_s/N$.2.3, 3.2
Frequency resolutionSmallest frequency spacing you can truly distinguish: $\Delta f = f_s/N$ (set by record length).2.3, 3.2
AliasingHigh frequencies above Nyquist masquerading as lower ones because of undersampling.2.2
Anti-aliasing filterA lowpass filter applied before the ADC to remove content above Nyquist.2.2
Spectral foldbackAnother name for aliasing — the spectrum "folds" around the Nyquist frequency.2.2
Zero-order hold (ZOH)A DAC holds each sample constant until the next — gives a staircase output and a $\text{sinc}$ roll-off.2.4
Reconstruction filterAn analog lowpass after the DAC that smooths the staircase into a continuous signal.2.4

4 · Signal Quality

TermMeaningSee
SNRSignal-to-Noise Ratio — ratio of signal power to noise power, usually in dB.3.1
dB (decibel)Logarithmic ratio. Power: $10\log_{10}$; amplitude: $20\log_{10}$.3.1, 5.5
AveragingAveraging $M$ repeated measurements improves SNR by $\sqrt{M}$ (≈3 dB per doubling).3.1
Zero-paddingAppending zeros at the end before an FFT — denser frequency grid, same true resolution.3.2, 6.5
Spectral leakageEnergy smearing into neighbouring bins when a tone doesn't sit exactly on a bin.3.3
Window / taperA function multiplying the signal to taper its ends and reduce leakage (Hann, Hamming, Blackman…).3.3
Main lobe / side lobeA window's central peak vs. the smaller ripples around it — the resolution/leakage trade-off.3.3
InterpolationEstimating values between known samples (linear, spline, sinc).3.4

5 · Time–Frequency

TermMeaningSee
STFTShort-Time Fourier Transform — FFT of successive short windows to see how the spectrum changes over time.4.1
SpectrogramA 2-D plot (time × frequency × magnitude) — the magnitude of the STFT.4.1
Time–frequency trade-offA long window gives fine frequency but coarse time resolution, and vice versa (uncertainty principle).4.1, 4.2
Overlap / hopHow far consecutive STFT windows are shifted; more overlap = smoother spectrogram.4.1
WaveletA short, localized oscillation used as a flexible time–frequency basis (multi-resolution).4.2
CWT / DWTContinuous / Discrete Wavelet Transform — analyse a signal across scales (≈ frequency bands).4.2

6 · Filters & the Z-Domain

TermMeaningSee
FilterAn LTI system that passes some frequencies and attenuates others.5.1
Lowpass / Highpass / Bandpass / BandstopThe four basic responses — keep lows / keep highs / keep a band / reject a band (notch).5.1
FIRFinite Impulse Response — feedforward only, always stable, can have linear phase.5.1
IIRInfinite Impulse Response — uses feedback, efficient/sharp but can be unstable, non-linear phase.5.1
Passband / Stopband / Transition bandFrequencies kept / rejected / the roll-off region between them.5.1
RippleGain variation within the pass- or stopband (e.g. Chebyshev filters trade ripple for steepness).5.1
Linear phaseAll frequencies delayed equally (constant group delay) — preserves waveform shape.5.1
Butterworth / Chebyshev / EllipticClassic IIR designs: maximally flat / equiripple in one band / equiripple in both (steepest).5.1
Z-transformGeneralizes the DFT to complex $z$; turns difference equations into algebra.5.2
Unit circle$|z| = 1$ in the z-plane; evaluating $H(z)$ there gives the frequency response.5.2, 5.6
ROCRegion of Convergence — the set of $z$ where the Z-transform sum converges; decides causality/stability.5.2
Difference equationThe recipe relating output samples to current/past inputs and past outputs.5.3
Transfer function $H(z)$$Y(z)/X(z)$ — ratio of polynomials whose roots are the zeros (numerator) and poles (denominator).5.2, 5.6
Pole / ZeroValues of $z$ where $H(z)$ blows up (pole, boosts gain) or vanishes (zero, notches gain).5.6
StabilityA causal filter is stable iff all poles lie strictly inside the unit circle ($|p|<1$).5.6
Block diagramA visual wiring of adders, gains, and delays ($z^{-1}$) implementing a difference equation.5.4
Bode diagramMagnitude (dB) and phase plotted vs. frequency (log axis) — the standard response view.5.5
Group delay$-d\phi/d\omega$ — the time delay a filter imposes on each frequency.5.5

7 · Resampling

TermMeaningSee
ResamplingChanging the sample rate of a signal (up, down, or by a rational factor $L/M$).6.1
Upsampling / InterpolationRaising the rate by $L$ — zero-stuff then lowpass filter to fill in the new samples.6.2
Downsampling / DecimationLowering the rate by $M$ — lowpass filter first (anti-alias) then keep every $M$-th sample.6.3
Zero stuffingInserting zeros between samples (the first step of upsampling); creates spectral images.6.4
Spectral imageCopies of the baseband spectrum produced by zero stuffing; removed by the interpolation filter.6.4
Zero stuffing vs zero paddingStuffing = zeros between samples (changes rate); padding = zeros at the end (only DFT grid).6.5
Easy to confuse: Resolution (set by record length $N$) vs bin spacing (set by FFT length after zero-padding); zero stuffing (rate change) vs zero padding (grid only); decimation (filter then drop samples) vs naive subsampling (drop samples → aliasing).

Quick Self-Check

Q1. What sets the true frequency resolution of a spectrum?

  • The FFT length after zero-padding
  • The record length $N$ (observation time), giving $\Delta f = f_s/N$
  • The window function used
  • The sample rate alone

Q2. A causal digital filter is stable when:

  • All zeros are inside the unit circle
  • All poles are strictly inside the unit circle
  • It has more zeros than poles
  • It is an FIR filter only

Q3. A spectrogram is best described as:

  • The FFT of the whole signal at once
  • The magnitude of the STFT — frequency content vs. time
  • The pole/zero plot of a filter
  • The autocorrelation of the signal
