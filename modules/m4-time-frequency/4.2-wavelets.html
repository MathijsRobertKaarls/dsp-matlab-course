4.2 — Wavelets

Module 4 · Time-Frequency Analysis · ~25 min

Introduction

The STFT uses the same window length at every frequency — a fixed-size "magnifying glass" sliding across time. Wavelets break this limitation by using windows that automatically shrink at high frequencies and grow at low frequencies. This gives high time resolution where it's needed (fast events, high frequencies) and high frequency resolution where it's needed (slow events, low frequencies) — simultaneously.

This multi-resolution analysis is why wavelets are used in JPEG2000, ECG denoising, seismology, and financial time-series analysis.

Core Concept

The Mother Wavelet

A wavelet transform is built from a single prototype function — the mother wavelet $\psi(t)$. All wavelets used in the transform are scaled (stretched/compressed) and shifted (translated) versions of it:

$$\psi_{a,b}(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{a}} \psi\!\left(\frac{t-b}{a}\right)$$

where $a > 0$ is the scale (large $a$ = stretched = low frequency) and $b$ is the translation (time position).

CWT vs DWT

  • Continuous Wavelet Transform (CWT): computes correlation with wavelets at all scales and positions. Produces a highly redundant, continuous time-scale map. Used for analysis and visualization.
  • Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT): uses a dyadic (powers of 2) scale grid. Fast, non-redundant, and perfect for denoising and compression.

DWT: Filterbank Interpretation

The DWT can be implemented as a cascade of highpass and lowpass filters followed by downsampling by 2 — the "Mallat algorithm":

  • Highpass → detail coefficients (high-frequency content)
  • Lowpass → approximation coefficients (low-frequency content, passed to next level)

Mathematical Foundation

Continuous Wavelet Transform:

$$W_x(a, b) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{a}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)\, \psi^*\!\left(\frac{t-b}{a}\right) dt$$

Interpretation: $W_x(a, b)$ measures how much the signal $x(t)$ resembles the wavelet at scale $a$ centered at time $b$. Large $|W_x|$ = strong match.

Popular mother wavelets:

  • Morlet: Gaussian-modulated sinusoid — great for frequency analysis.
  • Haar: step function — simplest, used in compression.
  • Daubechies (dbN): compact, smooth — most popular for DWT denoising.
  • Symlets, Coiflets: modifications of Daubechies with better symmetry.

Conceptual Questions

Q1. The key advantage of wavelet analysis over STFT is:

  • Wavelets are always faster to compute
  • Adaptive time-frequency resolution: high time resolution at high frequencies, high frequency resolution at low frequencies
  • Wavelets eliminate spectral leakage completely
  • Wavelets work without needing a window function

Q2. In the CWT, a large scale $a$ corresponds to:

  • High-frequency components
  • Low-frequency components (stretched wavelet)
  • Better time resolution
  • A larger hop size

Q3. In the Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT), the detail coefficients capture:

  • The overall shape (low-frequency approximation)
  • The high-frequency content at each decomposition level
  • The phase information only
  • The DC component

MATLAB Exercise

⚠ Requires Wavelet Toolbox. Verify with: license('test','Wavelet_Toolbox'). Octave users can use the wavelets package. 
%% 4.2 CWT and DWT

fs = 1000;
T  = 2;
t  = 0:1/fs:T-1/fs;

%% Part A — CWT of a chirp
x_chirp = chirp(t, 10, T, 400);

figure('Name','CWT of Chirp');
cwt(x_chirp, fs);
title('CWT Scalogram — Chirp (10→400 Hz)');
colormap('jet');

%% Part B — CWT of non-stationary signal
x_ns = [sin(2*pi*50*t(1:fs)), ...    % 50 Hz, 1st second
        sin(2*pi*200*t(1:fs))];        % 200 Hz, 2nd second
t_ns = (0:length(x_ns)-1)/fs;

figure('Name','CWT — Frequency Change');
subplot(2,1,1); plot(t_ns, x_ns); xlabel('Time (s)'); title('50 Hz → 200 Hz signal');
subplot(2,1,2); cwt(x_ns, fs); title('CWT reveals when frequency changes');

%% Part C — DWT for denoising
rng(1);
x_clean = sin(2*pi*30*t);
x_noisy = x_clean + 0.8*randn(size(t));

[C, L] = wavedec(x_noisy, 5, 'db4');   % 5-level decomposition with Daubechies-4

% Soft thresholding (denoising)
sigma = median(abs(C)) / 0.6745;        % Robust noise estimate
thr   = sigma * sqrt(2*log(length(x_noisy)));
C_thr = wthresh(C, 's', thr);          % Soft threshold
x_denoised = waverec(C_thr, L, 'db4'); % Reconstruct

figure('Name','DWT Denoising');
subplot(3,1,1); plot(t, x_clean); title('Clean signal'); ylim([-2 2]);
subplot(3,1,2); plot(t, x_noisy); title('Noisy signal'); ylim([-3 3]);
subplot(3,1,3); plot(t, x_denoised); hold on; plot(t, x_clean,'r--');
title(sprintf('Denoised (db4, 5 levels) — RMS error: %.3f', rms(x_denoised-x_clean)));
legend('Denoised','True'); ylim([-2 2]);

%% Part D — DWT coefficient visualisation
figure('Name','DWT Decomposition');
[C, L] = wavedec(x_noisy, 4, 'db4');
for lvl = 1:4
  d = detcoef(C, L, lvl);
  subplot(4,1,5-lvl);
  plot(d); title(sprintf('Detail coefficients — Level %d (%.0f–%.0f Hz)', lvl, fs/2^(lvl+1), fs/2^lvl));
end
💡 Hint

The CWT scalogram shows frequency (y-axis) vs time (x-axis). The chirp appears as a diagonal line sweeping from low to high frequency. The denoised signal in Part C should closely follow the clean original — DWT denoising is very effective for smooth signals in noisy backgrounds.

Key Takeaways

  • Wavelets overcome STFT's fixed resolution by using adaptive window sizes: narrow at high freq, wide at low freq.
  • The CWT produces a continuous scalogram — redundant, good for visualization.
  • The DWT uses a dyadic filterbank — fast, non-redundant, ideal for denoising & compression.
  • The mother wavelet $\psi$ is scaled ($a$) and translated ($b$) to generate all basis functions.
  • In MATLAB: cwt(x, fs) for CWT, wavedec(x, N, 'db4') for DWT.
