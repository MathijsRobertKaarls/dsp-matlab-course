5.2 — The Z-Transform

Module 5 · Filter Design · ~25 min

Introduction

The Z-transform is to discrete-time systems what the Laplace transform is to continuous-time systems. It converts difference equations into algebraic equations, making filter analysis and design elegant. It's the mathematical foundation for understanding stability, frequency response, and the relationship between filter coefficients and filter behavior.

Core Concept

Definition

The Z-transform of a sequence $x[n]$ is:

$$X(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x[n]\, z^{-n}$$

where $z$ is a complex variable. Think of $z^{-1}$ as a unit delay operator — multiplying by $z^{-1}$ delays the signal by one sample.

Region of Convergence (ROC)

The Z-transform only converges for certain values of $z$. The ROC is the set of $z$ where the sum converges. Key rules:

  • Causal systems: ROC is the exterior of a circle (outside some radius)
  • Anti-causal systems: ROC is the interior of a circle
  • A system is stable if the ROC includes the unit circle $|z| = 1$

The Unit Circle and Frequency Response

Evaluating $H(z)$ on the unit circle ($z = e^{j\omega}$) gives the DTFT — i.e., the frequency response:

$$H(e^{j\omega}) = H(z)\bigg|_{z=e^{j\omega}}$$

This is the critical link: the Z-transform evaluated on the unit circle is exactly the frequency response of the filter.

Transfer Function

For an LTI system described by coefficients $(b_k, a_k)$, the transfer function is:

$$H(z) = \frac{B(z)}{A(z)} = \frac{\sum_{k=0}^{M} b_k z^{-k}}{1 + \sum_{k=1}^{N} a_k z^{-k}}$$

The roots of $B(z) = 0$ are the zeros; the roots of $A(z) = 0$ are the poles. The locations of poles and zeros in the Z-plane determine the filter's entire frequency response and stability.

Mathematical Foundation

Common Z-transform pairs

$x[n]$ $X(z)$ ROC
$\delta[n]$ $1$ All $z$
$u[n]$ (unit step) $\frac{1}{1-z^{-1}}$ $|z| > 1$
$a^n u[n]$ $\frac{1}{1-az^{-1}}$ $|z| > |a|$
$\delta[n-k]$ $z^{-k}$ $z \neq 0$

Z-transform properties

  • Time shift: $x[n-k] \leftrightarrow z^{-k} X(z)$ — delay by $k$ = multiply by $z^{-k}$
  • Convolution: $x * h \leftrightarrow X(z) \cdot H(z)$ — convolution → multiplication
  • Linearity: $ax + by \leftrightarrow aX(z) + bY(z)$

MATLAB Exercise

%% 5.2 Z-Transform and Transfer Function Analysis

%% Part A — Simple pole-zero analysis
% First-order IIR: y[n] = x[n] + 0.9*y[n-1]
% Transfer function: H(z) = 1 / (1 - 0.9*z^{-1})
b = [1];
a = [1, -0.9];   % MATLAB convention: a(1)=1, rest are -a_k coefficients

figure('Name','Z-Plane and Frequency Response');
subplot(1,2,1);
zplane(b, a);     % Pole-zero plot on the unit circle
title('Pole-Zero Plot'); grid on;

subplot(1,2,2);
freqz(b, a, 512);
title('Frequency Response');

%% Part B — Compare different pole locations
pole_radii = [0.5, 0.8, 0.95, 1.0];  % last one is unstable!

figure('Name','Effect of Pole Radius on Frequency Response');
for i = 1:4
  r = pole_radii(i);
  a_i = [1, -r];
  subplot(2,2,i);
  if r < 1.0
    [H, f] = freqz([1], a_i, 512, 1000);
    plot(f, 20*log10(abs(H)));
    title(sprintf('Pole at r=%.2f (stable)', r));
  else
    title(sprintf('Pole at r=%.2f → UNSTABLE', r));
    text(0.5, 0.5, 'Diverges!', 'HorizontalAlignment','center','Units','normalized','FontSize',14,'Color','r');
  end
  xlabel('Hz'); ylabel('dB'); grid on;
end

%% Part C — Bandpass filter via pole-zero placement
% Place two conjugate poles near the desired frequency (angular)
fs = 1000;
f0 = 100;   % desired center frequency
omega0 = 2*pi*f0/fs;   % digital frequency
r = 0.95;   % pole radius (close to 1 = narrow, high-Q)

% Poles at r*e^{±jω₀}
poles = r * exp(1j*omega0);
poles = [poles; conj(poles)];

% Zeros at origin (for a simple design)
zeros_z = [0; 0];

[b_pz, a_pz] = zp2tf(zeros_z, poles, 1);

figure('Name','Pole-Zero Bandpass Design');
subplot(1,2,1); zplane(b_pz, a_pz); title(sprintf('Poles near %dHz (r=%.2f)', f0, r));
subplot(1,2,2); freqz(b_pz, a_pz, 1024, fs);
title(sprintf('Bandpass centered at %dHz', f0));

fprintf('\nPole locations:\n');
fprintf('  |p| = %.3f (should be < 1 for stability)\n', abs(roots(a_pz)));
💡 Hint

In Part B, notice how poles close to the unit circle (r=0.95) produce a very sharp, resonant peak in the frequency response. At r=1.0, the pole is ON the unit circle — the system is marginally unstable (oscillates forever). Part C demonstrates direct pole-zero design: placing conjugate poles near the unit circle at the desired frequency creates a bandpass filter.

Key Takeaways

  • Z-transform: $X(z) = \sum x[n] z^{-n}$. $z^{-1}$ = one-sample delay.
  • Transfer function $H(z) = B(z)/A(z)$; roots of $B$ = zeros, roots of $A$ = poles.
  • Frequency response = $H(z)$ evaluated on the unit circle: $H(e^{j\omega})$.
  • Stable ↔ all poles strictly inside the unit circle ($|p| < 1$).
  • In MATLAB: zplane(b,a) for pole-zero plot, freqz(b,a) for frequency response.
