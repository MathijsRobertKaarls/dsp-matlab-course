5.6 — Pole/Zero Plot

Module 5 · Filter Design · ~20 min

Introduction

Poles and zeros are the "personality" of a filter, plotted in the complex Z-plane. Their locations tell you everything: the frequency response, stability, and qualitative behavior — often without computing a single number. This geometric interpretation of filter behavior is powerful and elegant.

Core Concept

Poles and Zeros Defined

Given the transfer function $H(z) = \frac{B(z)}{A(z)}$:

  • Zeros $z_k$: values of $z$ where $B(z) = 0$ → $H(z_k) = 0$. Plotted as circles (○).
  • Poles $p_k$: values of $z$ where $A(z) = 0$ → $H(p_k) = \infty$. Plotted as crosses (×).

Geometric Frequency Response

The frequency response $H(e^{j\omega})$ can be computed geometrically. Evaluate $H(z)$ as you sweep around the unit circle at angle $\omega$:

  • A zero near the unit circle at angle $\omega_0$ → magnitude is small near $\omega_0$ (notch/dip).
  • A pole near the unit circle at angle $\omega_0$ → magnitude is large near $\omega_0$ (peak/resonance).
  • A zero ON the unit circle → exact null (infinite attenuation) at that frequency.

Stability from the Pole-Zero Plot

  • All poles inside the unit circle → BIBO stable.
  • Any pole on the unit circle → marginally stable (oscillates, doesn't grow or decay).
  • Any pole outside the unit circle → unstable (diverges).

FIR filters: all poles at origin

FIR filters have all their poles at $z = 0$ (the origin) — always inside the unit circle, always stable. Their response shape is controlled entirely by the zero locations.

Mathematical Foundation

The magnitude of the frequency response equals the product of distances from evaluation point $z = e^{j\omega}$ to all zeros, divided by the product of distances to all poles:

$$|H(e^{j\omega})| = |g| \cdot \frac{\prod_k |e^{j\omega} - z_k|}{\prod_k |e^{j\omega} - p_k|}$$

This geometric interpretation means:

  • When sweeping frequency ($\omega$ from 0 to $\pi$), the ratio of distances gives the magnitude.
  • A zero at $z = e^{j\omega_0}$ (on unit circle) → zero distance → $|H| = 0$ → perfect null at $f = \omega_0 f_s/(2\pi)$.

Conceptual Questions

Q1. Placing a zero exactly ON the unit circle at angle $\omega_0$ produces:

  • A resonance peak at that frequency
  • A perfect null (infinite attenuation) at the corresponding frequency
  • A −3dB cutoff at that frequency
  • Instability

Q2. Moving a pole closer to (but still inside) the unit circle causes the frequency response to:

  • Flatten out (more uniform gain)
  • Develop a sharper, taller peak at the pole's angular frequency
  • Become unstable immediately
  • Introduce a null at DC

Q3. Why are FIR filters unconditionally stable?

  • Their zeros are always on the unit circle
  • They have no zeros
  • All their poles are at $z=0$ (origin), always inside the unit circle
  • They use only real-valued coefficients

MATLAB Exercise

%% 5.6 Pole-Zero Plots and Geometric Frequency Response

fs = 1000;

%% Part A — Design various filters and examine pole-zero plots
figure('Name','Pole-Zero Plots');

filters = {
  butter(2, 0.3, 'low'),     'Butterworth LP'
  butter(4, 0.5, 'high'),    'Butterworth HP'
  butter(3, [0.2 0.5], 'bandpass'), 'Butterworth BP'
  butter(4, 0.3, 'low')      '4th order LP'
};

% Actually get b,a for each
[b1,a1] = butter(2, 0.3, 'low');
[b2,a2] = butter(4, 0.5, 'high');
[b3,a3] = butter(3, [0.2 0.5], 'bandpass');
[b4,a4] = ellip(4, 1, 40, 0.3, 'low');

ba = {{b1,a1,'Butterworth LP 2nd'},{b2,a2,'Butterworth HP 4th'},...
      {b3,a3,'Butterworth BP 3rd'},{b4,a4,'Elliptic LP 4th'}};

for i = 1:4
  subplot(2,4,i);
  zplane(ba{i}{1}, ba{i}{2});
  title(ba{i}{3}, 'FontSize', 8);

  subplot(2,4,i+4);
  freqz(ba{i}{1}, ba{i}{2}, 512, fs);
  title('');
  xlabel('Hz'); ylabel('dB');
end

%% Part B — Manual notch filter by pole-zero placement
% Notch at 60 Hz (power line noise) in 1000 Hz system
f_notch = 60;
omega_n = 2*pi*f_notch/fs;

% Place zeros ON unit circle at ±omega_n
z_zeros = exp(1j*omega_n) * [1; -1];   % wait — use conjugate pair
z_zeros = [exp(1j*omega_n); exp(-1j*omega_n)];

% Place poles slightly inside at same angle (to make narrow notch)
r_pole = 0.95;
z_poles = r_pole * [exp(1j*omega_n); exp(-1j*omega_n)];

[b_notch, a_notch] = zp2tf(z_zeros, z_poles, 1);
b_notch = real(b_notch);
a_notch = real(a_notch);

figure('Name','Manual Notch at 60Hz');
subplot(1,2,1); zplane(b_notch, a_notch); title('Pole-Zero: Notch at 60Hz');
subplot(1,2,2);
[H_n, f_n] = freqz(b_notch, a_notch, 4096, fs);
plot(f_n, 20*log10(abs(H_n)));
xlabel('Hz'); ylabel('dB'); title('Frequency response');
xline(f_notch, 'r--', '60Hz'); ylim([-60 5]); grid on;

%% Part C — Effect of pole/zero distance from unit circle
figure('Name','Pole Proximity Effect');
rs = [0.7, 0.85, 0.95, 0.99];
f_pole = 100;  omega_p = 2*pi*f_pole/fs;
for i = 1:4
  r = rs(i);
  p = r * [exp(1j*omega_p); exp(-1j*omega_p)];
  z = [0; 0];
  [bp, ap] = zp2tf(z, p, 1);
  [H, f] = freqz(real(bp), real(ap), 4096, fs);
  subplot(2,2,i);
  plot(f, 20*log10(abs(H)));
  title(sprintf('Pole r=%.2f at 100Hz', r));
  xlabel('Hz'); ylabel('dB'); xlim([0 500]); ylim([-10 40]); grid on;
end
💡 Hint

In Part C, watch how the peak at 100 Hz grows dramatically as the pole radius approaches 1.0. At r=0.7 you see a mild bump; at r=0.99 you see an extremely sharp, tall resonance peak (high Q). In Part B, the zeros ON the unit circle create exactly 0 gain (-∞ dB) at 60 Hz. The nearby poles ensure rapid recovery on either side.

Key Takeaways

  • Poles (×) near the unit circle create peaks; zeros (○) near the unit circle create dips.
  • Zero ON the unit circle → perfect null at that frequency.
  • Stability: all poles must be strictly inside the unit circle.
  • FIR = all poles at origin → always stable.
  • In MATLAB: zplane(b,a); zp2tf(zeros, poles, gain) for design from pole-zero locations.
