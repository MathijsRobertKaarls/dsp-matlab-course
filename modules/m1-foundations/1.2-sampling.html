1.2 — Sampling (ADC & DAC)

Module 1 · Foundations · ~20 min

Introduction

Imagine trying to describe a smooth curve by choosing a series of points on it. If you pick points far apart, you might miss key features of the curve — or worse, misrepresent it entirely. If you pick points very close together, you capture the shape accurately, but you have a lot of data to store.

This trade-off is sampling: converting a continuous analog signal into a sequence of discrete numbers. It is the gateway between the physical world and digital computers, performed by an Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC).

💡 Core Insight You don't need to sample infinitely fast. There's a minimum safe sampling rate — the Nyquist rate — that guarantees perfect reconstruction. Sample faster than that and you're safe. Sample slower and you get aliasing (covered in Topic 2.2).

Core Concept

The Nyquist–Shannon Sampling Theorem

This is arguably the most important result in all of DSP. It states:

A bandlimited signal with highest frequency component $f_{max}$ can be perfectly reconstructed from its samples, provided the sampling frequency satisfies:
$$f_s \geq 2 \cdot f_{max}$$

The minimum safe rate $2 \cdot f_{max}$ is called the Nyquist rate. Half of the sampling frequency, $f_{Nyquist} = f_s / 2$, is called the Nyquist frequency — this is the highest frequency your system can represent.

Quantization

Sampling gives you the timing of measurements. But values must also be rounded to a finite set of levels — this is quantization. An ADC with $B$ bits maps signal amplitude to one of $2^B$ discrete levels.

  • 8-bit audio: 256 levels — noticeably "crunchy"
  • 16-bit CD audio: 65,536 levels — smooth, clean
  • 24-bit studio: 16.7 million levels — professional grade

The rounding error introduced by quantization is called quantization noise. Each extra bit adds ~6 dB of signal-to-noise ratio.

Mathematical Foundation

Sampling

Mathematically, sampling multiplies the continuous signal $x_a(t)$ by an impulse train:

$$x[n] = x_a(n \cdot T_s) = x_a\!\left(\frac{n}{f_s}\right)$$

Quantization

With $B$ bits and signal range $[-A, +A]$, the step size (resolution) is:

$$\Delta = \frac{2A}{2^B - 1}$$

The quantized value is the nearest representable level. The maximum quantization error is $\pm \Delta/2$.

Signal-to-Quantization-Noise Ratio (SQNR)

For a full-scale sinusoidal input:

$$\text{SQNR} \approx 6.02 \cdot B + 1.76 \;\text{dB}$$

Every additional bit of resolution adds approximately 6 dB of SQNR.

Example: 16-bit audio: SQNR ≈ 6.02 × 16 + 1.76 ≈ 98 dB — that's why CD audio sounds clean.

MATLAB Exercise

We'll simulate sampling at different rates and observe quantization.

%% 1.2 Sampling and Quantization Demo

% --- Part A: Effect of sampling rate ---
fs_high = 1000;   % High sampling rate (safe)
fs_low  = 150;    % Low sampling rate (may alias — try values below 200)
f0 = 100;         % Signal frequency (Hz)
T  = 0.05;        % Duration (s)

t_high = 0:1/fs_high:T;
t_low  = 0:1/fs_low:T;
t_cont = 0:1/10000:T;   % "Continuous" reference

x_cont  = sin(2*pi*f0*t_cont);
x_high  = sin(2*pi*f0*t_high);
x_low   = sin(2*pi*f0*t_low);

figure('Name','Sampling Rate Effect');
subplot(3,1,1); plot(t_cont, x_cont); title('Original (continuous)'); ylabel('x(t)');
subplot(3,1,2); stem(t_high, x_high, 'filled'); title(sprintf('Sampled at %d Hz (safe)', fs_high));
subplot(3,1,3); stem(t_low,  x_low,  'filled'); title(sprintf('Sampled at %d Hz', fs_low));
xlabel('Time (s)');

% --- Part B: Quantization effect ---
fs = 8000; t = 0:1/fs:0.5;
x = sin(2*pi*440*t);

bits_list = [2, 4, 8, 16];
figure('Name','Quantization Effect');
for k = 1:4
  B = bits_list(k);
  levels = 2^B;
  % Quantize: scale to [0, levels-1], round, scale back to [-1,1]
  x_q = round((x + 1) / 2 * (levels - 1)) / (levels - 1) * 2 - 1;
  subplot(2,2,k);
  plot(t(1:200), x(1:200), '--', 'Color', [0.3 0.7 1]);
  hold on;
  plot(t(1:200), x_q(1:200), 'Color', [0.5 0.2 0.9]);
  title(sprintf('%d-bit (%d levels)', B, levels));
  ylim([-1.2 1.2]); ylabel('Amplitude');
  legend('Original','Quantized','Location','northeast');
end
💡 Hint

In Part A, compare the low-rate stem plot to the original. Can you still identify the 100 Hz wave from the sparse samples? In Part B, at 2-bit quantization you'll see a very coarse "staircase" approximation; by 16 bits the quantized signal is indistinguishable from the original in the plot.

Key Takeaways

  • Sampling converts continuous signals to discrete sequences; $f_s = 1/T_s$.
  • The Nyquist theorem: sample at $f_s \geq 2 f_{max}$ for perfect reconstruction.
  • Quantization maps each sample to a finite number of levels; $B$ bits → $2^B$ levels.
  • Each extra bit of resolution adds ~6 dB of SQNR.
  • ADC = analog → digital; DAC = digital → analog.
