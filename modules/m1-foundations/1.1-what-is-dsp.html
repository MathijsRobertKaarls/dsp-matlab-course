1.1 — What is Digital Signal Processing?

Module 1 · Foundations · ~15 min

Introduction

Imagine you're on a phone call. Your voice travels as sound waves through the air, gets converted to electrical variations, then turned into a stream of numbers, compressed, transmitted across the internet, reconstructed, and finally played back as sound — all in milliseconds. That entire chain? It's Digital Signal Processing (DSP) in action.

In the simplest terms: a signal is information that changes over time (or space). DSP is the art and science of manipulating that information once it's represented as numbers.

💡 Key Idea Analog signals are continuous — they can take any value at any instant. Digital signals are discrete — they're a list of numbers sampled at regular moments in time. DSP works in the digital world.

Core Concept

What is a signal?

A signal is any quantity that carries information and varies with respect to one or more independent variables. The most common case is variation over time, but signals can also vary over space (images), frequency, or other dimensions.

Real-world examples you already know:

  • Audio — air pressure varying with time (speech, music)
  • ECG / EKG — electrical voltage from your heart over time
  • Wi-Fi / Radio — electromagnetic waves carrying encoded data
  • Seismograph — ground vibration amplitude over time
  • Image sensor — light intensity varying across a 2D grid

Analog vs. Digital

The physical world is analog — temperature, pressure, voltage, all vary continuously. But computers can only work with discrete numbers. The transformation from analog to digital (and back) is a central concern of DSP.

What does "processing" mean?

Once you have a sequence of numbers (your digital signal), you can:

  • Filter — remove noise, isolate a frequency band
  • Transform — see which frequencies are present (Fourier Transform)
  • Compress — reduce data size (MP3, JPEG)
  • Detect — find patterns (radar target detection, ECG anomaly)
  • Synthesize — generate new signals (speech synthesis, music)

Mathematical Foundation

A digital signal is represented as a sequence:

$$x[n], \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, N-1$$

where:

  • $x$ — the signal (e.g. recorded audio)
  • $n$ — the sample index (integer, dimensionless)
  • $N$ — total number of samples
  • $x[n]$ — the value of the signal at sample $n$ (a real or complex number)

The sampling period $T_s$ (in seconds) and sampling frequency $f_s$ (in Hz) are related by:

$$T_s = \frac{1}{f_s}$$

The actual time corresponding to sample $n$ is: $t = n \cdot T_s$

Example: CD audio is sampled at $f_s = 44100$ Hz. That means $T_s \approx 22.7\,\mu s$ — one new number every 22.7 microseconds. A 3-minute song contains about 7.9 million samples per channel.

MATLAB Exercise

Let's generate and play your first digital signal — a simple sine wave.

%% 1.1 Your first digital signal
% Sampling parameters
fs = 8000;          % Sampling frequency (Hz) — like a low-quality phone call
T  = 1;             % Duration in seconds
t  = 0:1/fs:T-1/fs; % Time vector: N = fs*T = 8000 samples

% Generate a 440 Hz sine wave (concert pitch A)
f0 = 440;           % Signal frequency in Hz
x  = sin(2*pi*f0*t);

% Plot it
figure;
subplot(2,1,1);
plot(t, x);
xlabel('Time (s)'); ylabel('Amplitude');
title('440 Hz sine wave — full 1 second');
xlim([0 0.01]);     % Zoom to first 10 ms so you can see the wave

subplot(2,1,2);
stem(0:39, x(1:40), 'filled', 'MarkerSize', 3);
xlabel('Sample index n'); ylabel('x[n]');
title('First 40 samples as discrete sequence');

% Play it (requires speakers/headphones)
sound(x, fs);
💡 Hint — What should you observe?

The top plot shows a smooth-looking wave over 10 ms — you'll see about 4.4 full cycles (440 cycles/s × 0.01 s). The bottom stem plot shows individual sample values as dots. Each dot is one number stored in memory. Between the dots there is no information — that's the fundamental nature of a digital signal.

Exercise Questions

  1. How many total samples are in the array x? Check with length(x) in MATLAB.
  2. Change fs to 44100 (CD quality). How many samples do you get now?
  3. Change f0 to 880. How does the sound change? What changes in the plot?

Expected outputs

  • fs=8000, T=1: length(x) = 8000
  • fs=44100, T=1: length(x) = 44100
  • At 880 Hz the tone is one octave higher; you'll see twice as many wave cycles in the same time window.

Key Takeaways

  • A signal is information that varies over time (or space).
  • A digital signal is a sequence of discrete numbers $x[n]$ sampled at rate $f_s$.
  • Sampling frequency $f_s = 1/T_s$ determines how many numbers represent one second of signal.
  • DSP manipulates these numbers mathematically — filtering, transforming, compressing, detecting.
  • Real-world applications span audio, communications, biomedical, radar, and image processing.
